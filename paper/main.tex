\begin{abstract}
We empirically study how two key hyperparameters of contrastive self-supervised 
learning --- InfoNCE temperature $\tau$ and negative batch size --- shape the 
geometry of the learned embedding space. Training SimCLR with a ResNet-18 backbone 
on CIFAR-10, we measure uniformity, alignment, and dimensional collapse (via 
effective rank) across five temperature values ($\tau \in \{0.07, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 
1.0\}$) and four batch sizes ($N \in \{128, 256, 512, 1024\}$). We find that low 
temperatures produce highly uniform representations with high effective rank but 
poor alignment, while high temperatures cause severe dimensional collapse --- 
reducing the effective rank from 88 to 12. Batch size independently controls 
representation richness: doubling the batch size from 128 to 1024 increases 
effective rank from 76 to 99 with minimal impact on uniformity. Notably, the 
configuration with the highest effective rank ($\tau=0.07$) does not achieve the 
best downstream accuracy, suggesting that geometric quality and task performance 
are not perfectly aligned. These results provide empirical grounding for 
hyperparameter selection in contrastive learning pipelines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Contrastive self-supervised learning has emerged as a dominant paradigm for 
learning visual representations without labels~\cite{chen2020simple}. At its 
core, these methods optimize the InfoNCE loss~\cite{chen2020simple}, which 
encourages representations of augmented views of the same image to be similar 
(\emph{alignment}) while pushing apart representations of different images 
(\emph{uniformity})~\cite{wang2020understanding}.

Despite their empirical success, the effect of key hyperparameters on the 
\emph{geometry} of the learned embedding space remains underexplored. The 
temperature $\tau$ controls the sharpness of the softmax distribution over 
negatives, and the batch size determines how many negatives are available per 
update. Both are known to affect downstream accuracy, but their relationship 
to geometric properties such as dimensional collapse is less understood.

Dimensional collapse --- the phenomenon where learned representations span only 
a small subspace of the available embedding dimensions --- has been identified 
as a key failure mode in self-supervised learning~\cite{hua2021feature}. A 
collapsed representation discards potentially useful information, limiting 
transferability to downstream tasks.

In this work, we conduct a controlled empirical study of how $\tau$ and batch 
size affect the geometry of SimCLR representations on CIFAR-10. We track 
uniformity, alignment, and effective rank throughout training, and evaluate 
downstream quality via linear probe accuracy. Our main findings are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Low $\tau$ produces high uniformity and high effective rank, but 
    at the cost of poor alignment between positive pairs.
    \item High $\tau$ causes severe dimensional collapse, reducing effective 
    rank from 88 to 12.
    \item Larger batch sizes independently increase effective rank with 
    minimal effect on uniformity.
    \item The best downstream accuracy is achieved at $\tau=0.2$, not at 
    the lowest $\tau$, revealing a non-trivial tradeoff between geometric 
    quality and task performance.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}

\subsection{InfoNCE Loss}

Given a batch of $N$ images, SimCLR~\cite{chen2020simple} generates two augmented 
views of each image and encodes them through a backbone network followed by a 
projection head, yielding normalized embeddings $z_i, z_j \in \mathbb{S}^{d-1}$. 
The NT-Xent (InfoNCE) loss for a positive pair $(i, j)$ is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau)}
    {\sum_{k \neq i} \exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_k)/\tau)}
\end{equation}

where $\text{sim}(u, v) = u^\top v / \|u\|\|v\|$ is the cosine similarity and 
$\tau > 0$ is the temperature hyperparameter. Lower $\tau$ sharpens the 
distribution over negatives, making the loss focus on hard negatives.

\subsection{Uniformity and Alignment}

Wang and Isola~\cite{wang2020understanding} decompose representation quality 
into two complementary properties. \textbf{Alignment} measures how close 
positive pairs are on the unit hypersphere:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{align} = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim p_\text{pos}} 
  \left[\|f(x) - f(y)\|^2\right]
\end{equation}

\textbf{Uniformity} measures how evenly representations are distributed on 
the hypersphere, using a Gaussian kernel:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{unif} = \log\, \mathbb{E}_{x,y \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}
  {\sim} p_\text{data}} \left[e^{-2\|f(x)-f(y)\|^2}\right]
\end{equation}

Good representations should minimize both: aligned positive pairs and 
uniformly distributed embeddings. These two objectives are in tension --- 
perfect alignment would collapse all representations to a single point.

\subsection{Dimensional Collapse and Effective Rank}

Dimensional collapse occurs when learned representations span only a small 
subspace of $\mathbb{R}^d$~\cite{hua2021feature}. We quantify this via the 
\textbf{effective rank}~\cite{roy2007effective}, defined as the exponential 
of the Shannon entropy of the normalized singular value distribution of the 
embedding matrix $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$:

\begin{equation}
  \text{erank}(Z) = \exp\left(-\sum_i p_i \log p_i\right), 
  \quad p_i = \frac{\sigma_i}{\sum_j \sigma_j}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_i$ are the singular values of $Z$. A higher effective rank 
indicates a richer, less collapsed representation space, with maximum value 
$d$ (the embedding dimension).


\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Model and Training}

We train SimCLR~\cite{chen2020simple} with a ResNet-18 backbone and a 
two-layer MLP projection head (512 $\rightarrow$ 512 $\rightarrow$ 128) on 
CIFAR-10 (50,000 training images, 10 classes). All models are trained for 
200 epochs using the Adam optimizer with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, 
weight decay $10^{-4}$, and cosine annealing schedule.

The data augmentation pipeline follows the standard SimCLR protocol: random 
resized crop (scale 0.2--1.0), random horizontal flip, color jitter 
(brightness, contrast, saturation, hue), and random grayscale conversion.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sweeps}

We conduct two independent sweeps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temperature sweep:} $\tau \in \{0.07, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0\}$ 
    with fixed batch size $N=256$.
    \item \textbf{Batch size sweep:} $N \in \{128, 256, 512, 1024\}$ with 
    fixed temperature $\tau=0.1$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Geometry metrics are computed every 10 epochs on the projection head embeddings 
$z$, using 10 batches of training data. We compute:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Uniformity} and \textbf{alignment} as defined in 
    Section~\ref{sec:background}.
    \item \textbf{Effective rank} of the $N \times 128$ embedding matrix.
    \item \textbf{Top-5 normalized singular values} to characterize the 
    shape of dimensional collapse.
\end{itemize}

Downstream performance is evaluated via \textbf{linear probe}: a logistic 
regression classifier (C=0.1) trained on frozen backbone features extracted 
from the full CIFAR-10 training set, evaluated on the test set.

\subsection{Implementation}

All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA P100 GPU via Kaggle. Code is 
available at \url{https://github.com/nicolasallerponte/EmbeddingCollapseStudy}.

\subsection{Geometry vs.\ Downstream Performance}

A key finding is that geometric quality does not directly predict downstream 
accuracy. The configuration with the highest effective rank ($\tau=0.07$, 
erank=88) achieves only 71.3\% test accuracy, while $\tau=0.2$ (erank=67) 
achieves the best accuracy of 75.3\%. This suggests that moderate collapse 
may act as an implicit regularizer, discarding noise and retaining 
task-relevant structure.

\section{Discussion}

Our results reveal two distinct mechanisms by which hyperparameters shape 
contrastive representations.

\textbf{Temperature controls the uniformity-alignment tradeoff.} Low $\tau$ 
sharpens the softmax over negatives, effectively treating all negatives as 
hard negatives. This forces the model to spread representations uniformly 
across the hypersphere, maximizing effective rank. However, this comes at 
the cost of alignment --- positive pairs are not pulled close enough, 
degrading downstream transferability. High $\tau$ has the opposite effect: 
the loss becomes insensitive to individual negatives, allowing representations 
to collapse into a low-dimensional subspace while achieving good alignment.

\textbf{Batch size controls representation dimensionality.} More negatives 
per update provide a richer contrastive signal, forcing the model to maintain 
diverse representations across more dimensions. Crucially, this effect is 
largely orthogonal to uniformity --- the hyperspherical distribution remains 
stable while the effective rank grows. This suggests that batch size and 
temperature act on different geometric axes of the embedding space.

\textbf{The geometry-performance gap.} Perhaps the most practically relevant 
finding is that the best downstream accuracy ($\tau=0.2$, 75.3\%) does not 
coincide with the best geometry ($\tau=0.1$, erank=89). Representations 
with very high effective rank may encode too much low-level variation, while 
moderate collapse may implicitly filter out task-irrelevant dimensions. This 
challenges the assumption that geometric quality metrics are sufficient 
proxies for representation quality, and suggests that both perspectives are 
necessary for a complete evaluation.

\textbf{Limitations.} This study is limited to CIFAR-10 with a ResNet-18 
backbone and a single projection dimension of 128. The batch size sweep 
fixes $\tau=0.1$, leaving the interaction between both hyperparameters 
unexplored. Future work should extend these findings to larger datasets 
(e.g., STL-10, ImageNet-100), higher-capacity backbones, and joint sweeps 
over both hyperparameters simultaneously.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented an empirical study of how InfoNCE temperature and batch size 
affect the geometry of contrastive representations. Our key findings are: 
(1) temperature governs the uniformity-alignment tradeoff and is the primary 
driver of dimensional collapse; (2) batch size independently controls 
effective rank with minimal effect on hyperspherical uniformity; and (3) 
geometric quality metrics are imperfect proxies for downstream performance, 
with the best accuracy achieved at intermediate temperature rather than at 
the geometric optimum. These results provide practical guidance for 
hyperparameter selection in contrastive learning and motivate further 
investigation into the relationship between representation geometry and 
downstream transferability.
